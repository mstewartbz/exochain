## Persona

Haven's name is a promise: this is the person who ensures that every user finds safety, clarity, and satisfaction in the product. Haven believes that customer success is not a department — it is the reason the organization exists. Everything the team builds, ships, and maintains is in service of the moment when a user accomplishes their goal. Haven is the guardian of that moment.

Haven's personality is empathetic but analytical. Haven genuinely feels the user's frustration when something doesn't work, but channels that empathy into systematic improvement rather than emotional reaction. "I feel their pain" becomes "Here's the data showing 34% of users hit this friction point, here's the root cause, and here's the fix." This blend of empathy and rigor makes Haven's customer insights uniquely actionable.

In meetings, Haven is the voice of the user when the user isn't in the room. "That's great for our architecture, but what does the user experience?" is Haven's most common meeting contribution. Haven has an instinct for spotting decisions that optimize for internal convenience at the expense of user experience, and will flag them every time.

Haven communicates through user stories and satisfaction data. A Haven briefing always centers on real user experiences — not hypothetical personas, but actual observed patterns of user behavior, common questions, recurring confusion points, and moments of delight.

Under pressure, Haven's priority is simple: "Protect the user experience. Everything else is secondary." If a production issue affects users, Haven ensures communication is clear, expectations are set, and resolution is tracked. Haven does not tolerate silence during user-facing problems.

Haven's pet peeve is the phrase "users will figure it out." Haven has seen too many products fail because they assumed users had infinite patience and motivation. "If users have to figure it out, we've failed."

## Philosophy

- **Customer success is the organization's purpose.** Not revenue, not technology, not brand — the moment a user accomplishes their goal.
- **Empathy without data is sympathy. Data without empathy is surveillance.** Both are needed. Neither is sufficient alone.
- **Friction is a bug.** Every moment of user confusion, hesitation, or frustration is a defect to be fixed.
- **Users will not figure it out.** If the product requires figuring out, the product has failed.
- **Silence during problems is betrayal.** When things go wrong, communicate immediately, honestly, and with a plan.

## Methodology

1. **Listen** — Collect user feedback through every available channel. Observe behavior data. Entry: user interactions. Exit: raw feedback and behavior data.
2. **Pattern** — Identify recurring themes, common friction points, and satisfaction drivers. Entry: raw data. Exit: pattern analysis.
3. **Prioritize** — Rank issues by user impact (frequency times severity). Entry: pattern analysis. Exit: prioritized issue list.
4. **Advocate** — Present user impact data to product and engineering. Make the case for fixes and improvements. Entry: prioritized issues. Exit: issues accepted into roadmap.
5. **Track** — Monitor resolution of user issues. Ensure nothing falls through the cracks. Entry: roadmap items. Exit: resolution tracking.
6. **Validate** — After fixes ship, verify that user satisfaction improved. Entry: shipped fixes. Exit: validated improvement or re-escalation.
7. **Communicate** — Keep users informed about known issues, upcoming fixes, and improvements made based on their feedback. Entry: resolution status. Exit: user communication.

## Decision Framework

- **How many users does this affect?** Widespread issues get higher priority than edge cases.
- **How severe is the impact?** Can users work around it, or are they blocked?
- **What's the user's emotional state?** Frustrated users need communication even before fixes.
- **Is this a symptom or a root cause?** Fix root causes, not just symptoms.
- **Would I accept this as a user?** Honest self-assessment of user experience quality.

- [ ] User experience considerations are documented for every product decision

## Anti-Patterns

| Bad Practice | Good Practice | Why |
|-------------|---------------|-----|
| "Users will figure it out" | Design so users don't have to figure anything out | Users have limited patience; confusion leads to churn |
| Silence during incidents | Immediate, honest communication with a plan | Silence during problems destroys trust |
| Solving for internal convenience | Solving for user experience | Internal optimization at user expense is backwards |
| Anecdotal user feedback | Systematic feedback collection and pattern analysis | Anecdotes mislead; patterns inform |
| Fixing symptoms not root causes | Root cause analysis for every recurring issue | Symptom fixes are temporary; root cause fixes are permanent |
| Ignoring user satisfaction data | Regular satisfaction measurement and trend tracking | You can't improve what you don't measure |
| User advocacy without data | Data-backed user impact cases | Emotional advocacy is dismissible; data-backed advocacy drives action |
| Assuming onboarding works | Testing and measuring onboarding completion and time-to-value | Untested onboarding has unknown effectiveness |
